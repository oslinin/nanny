# Child Guidance (evidence-based reference notes)

These are original, plain-language summaries of mainstream, evidence-based
guidance — not verbatim excerpts of any source. Each note states what
reputable public-health bodies broadly agree on, so an answer can be grounded
in consensus rather than a single opinion or a random internet claim. Cite the
relevant source(s) named below when you use a note, and see `SOURCES.md` for
links and licenses.

Ground rules for using these notes:

- These are general education, **not medical advice, diagnosis, or a care
  plan**. Frame anything derived from them as "a pattern worth discussing with
  your pediatrician," never as a conclusion about the child's health.
- Prefer these consensus notes over anything a live search surfaces if they
  conflict. If a question falls outside these notes and no configured tool
  gives a reputable, citable answer, say you don't have enough evidence rather
  than inventing one.
- Tie every point back to the specific activity that was logged. Ranges below
  are typical, not targets — normal healthy babies vary widely.

## Feeding

- **Newborn frequency.** Young infants typically feed frequently — often
  around 8–12 times per 24 hours in the early weeks — because their stomachs
  are small; feeding on cue (hunger signs) rather than a fixed clock is the
  broadly recommended approach. (UNICEF; CDC; AAP)
- **Bottle volumes.** Formula- or expressed-milk intake per feed generally
  rises as a baby grows; overfeeding signs include lots of spit-up and
  discomfort. Paced bottle-feeding (bottle near-horizontal, brief pauses) lets
  a baby self-regulate. (CDC; AAP)
- **Introducing solids.** Around 6 months is the commonly cited window to
  begin complementary foods *alongside* continued milk feeds, once a baby can
  sit with support and shows interest. Introduce single new foods a few days
  apart so any reaction is easier to spot. (UNICEF; CDC; AAP; USDA/FNS)
- **Iron & variety.** From ~6 months, iron-rich first foods matter because
  in-utero iron stores decline; a gradually widening variety of textures and
  food groups is encouraged. (CDC; USDA/FNS)

## Diapering as a hydration signal

- **Wet diapers.** After the first week, roughly 6 or more wet diapers a day
  is a commonly used rough indicator of adequate intake; a clear drop can be an
  early sign of underfeeding or dehydration worth raising promptly. (UNICEF;
  AAP)
- **Stools.** Stool frequency and consistency vary enormously between normal
  babies and shift with diet (breastmilk vs formula vs solids). A single
  unusual diaper is rarely meaningful; a persistent change in pattern is what's
  worth mentioning. (AAP)

## Sleep

- **Amounts.** Total daily sleep is high in infancy and decreases with age,
  spread across night sleep and naps; wide variation between babies is normal.
  (CDC; AAP)
- **Safe sleep.** The consistent public-health message is back-to-sleep, on a
  firm flat surface, with no loose bedding — relevant context whenever sleep
  comes up, though this tracker logs feeds/diapers rather than sleep position.
  (AAP; CDC)

## Development & responsive care

- **Responsive caregiving.** Talking, reading, and responsive back-and-forth
  interaction during everyday routines (including feeds) support early
  language, cognitive, and social-emotional development — a core theme of
  UNICEF's parenting guidance. (UNICEF; OpenStax *Lifespan Development*)
- **Milestones are ranges.** Developmental milestones are described as typical
  age ranges, not deadlines; steady progress matters more than any single date,
  and concerns are best checked at routine well-child visits. (CDC "Learn the
  Signs. Act Early."; OpenStax *Lifespan Development*)

## When to point toward a professional

Encourage contacting a pediatrician (not this app) for: a sustained drop in wet
diapers or feeding, poor weight gain, fever in a young infant, persistent
vomiting or diarrhea, or any pattern that worries the caregiver. This tool
surfaces patterns; it does not evaluate them clinically. (AAP; CDC)
